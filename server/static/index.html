What Is This?

You write a Python bot that buys stuff on a simulated stock market. Your bot needs to buy 10,000 units over 500 time steps. The catch: every time you buy, you push the price up. And everyone else's bots are buying at the same time, fighting over the same limited supply.

The dumb approach — buying everything at once — gets crushed because you eat through all the cheap offers and end up paying way more. The game is figuring out when to buy, how much to buy, and what price to offer.

How Scoring Works

The starting price is $100. Your score is how much more (or less) you paid on average compared to that starting price.

A score of 50 bps means your average buy price was $100.50 — you overpaid by $0.50 per unit, or $5,000 total. A score of -10 bps means you averaged $99.90 — you actually beat the starting price. Lower is better. Negative is amazing.

Each tournament runs your bot 50 times with different random market conditions and averages the results, so you can't just get lucky once.

The Market

The market simulates a single asset with a continuous order book. There are three types of participants:

Market Makers — quotes 10 price levels on each side of mid. Thinner at top, thicker deeper. Widens spread as cumulative agent buy volume grows.

Noise Traders — ~5 random orders per tick, 50/50 buy/sell. Can fill your resting limit orders. Mean-zero flow — they don't move the price directionally.

Player Agents (you) — all bots execute simultaneously. Every bot sees the same order book snapshot, then submits orders. Execution order among players is randomized each tick — if two bots want the same liquidity, who gets it first is random. Fair in expectation across 50 seeds.

Price Dynamics — the mid price follows a random walk (GBM). On top of that, aggressive agent volume pushes the price up permanently. Passive fills (your limit orders filled by noise traders) do not contribute to this flow signal — providing liquidity has less impact than consuming it.

Tick Sequence — each tick: (1) price updates, (2) market maker posts quotes, (3) noise traders execute, (4) all agents see the book and submit orders, (5) agent orders execute in random order, (6) unfilled orders cancel.

Every tick, your bot sees: the full order book (all prices and sizes), recent trades by all participants, your fill history, and your remaining quantity. You return a list of orders.

What Your Bot Can Do

Market Orders

Buy immediately at whatever price is available. Guaranteed to fill, but you pay the current asking price. If you buy a lot at once, you "walk the book" — eating through cheap offers and paying more for each additional unit.

Limit Orders

Place an offer to buy at a specific price. Only fills if someone sells to you at that price. Cheaper when it works, but might not fill at all. Unfilled limit orders are cancelled at the end of each tick.

Strategy Ideas

Spread it out — buy a little bit each tick instead of all at once. This is called TWAP (Time-Weighted Average Price) and it's the simplest strategy that actually works.

Use limit orders — instead of paying the asking price, post a buy order slightly below it. You get a better price when it fills, and you don't push the market up as much.

Read the book — if there's a lot of supply available at the current price, it's a good time to buy. If the book is thin, wait.

Watch the spread — the gap between the best buy and sell prices widens when the market detects aggressive buying. When the spread is wide, back off. When it tightens, buy.

Watch the tape — you can see all recent trades. If another bot just slammed the market, prices are temporarily elevated. Wait for things to settle.

Go full quant — train a reinforcement learning model offline, load the weights in your bot, and run inference each tick. The sky is the limit.

Getting Started

  1. Clone the repo: git clone https://github.com/Wesius/vibecoding-comps.git
  2. Install dependencies: uv sync  (don't have uv? curl -LsSf https://astral.sh/uv/install.sh | sh)
  3. Drop your credentials .txt file in the repo root and rename it to .env
  4. Edit agent/agent.py — this is your bot. It comes pre-filled with a basic strategy.
  5. Test locally: uv run python cli.py test — runs your bot against example bots on your machine
  6. Submit: uv run python cli.py submit — uploads your bot and runs a tournament immediately

You can submit as many times as you want. Each submit overwrites your previous bot and runs a fresh tournament. Tournaments also auto-run every 60 seconds. Iterate fast.

Look at the example bots in the agents/ folder for inspiration — naive.py, twap.py, vwap.py, and adaptive.py.
